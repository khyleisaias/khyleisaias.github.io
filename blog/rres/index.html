Comparison

Nature of Ultimate Reality
Christianity
  • God is not seen physically, but felt by it's supporters.
  • God is forgiving.
  • Have 3 personas: the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit.
Islam
  • Their ultimate reality is Allah.
  • Allah is unique.
Hinduism

    Their ultimate reality is central to it's philosophy and spiritual teachings. These are:

  1. Brahman - The supreme, formless, infinite, eternal reality, beyond all dualities and limitations.
  2. Atman - The individual self or soul, which is ultimately identical to Brahman.
  3. Moksha - Liberation from the cycle of samsara (birth, death, and rebirth), achieved through realization of Brahman.
  4. Maya - The illusion that creates the perception of separation between the self and Brahman.
  5. Advaita Vedanta - A non-dualistic school of Atman and Brahman.
  6. Tat Tvam Asi - A great saying from the Upanishads meaning "You are that".
Buddhism

They do not propose a single eternal ultimate reality.

Origin and Purpose of the World
Christianity

Christianity was started in Israel, and was based on the teachings of Jesus of Nazareth.

Islam

Islam was originated in Saudi Arabia from around 7th century. The aim is to praise only one God - Allah, and live a life around what's pleasing to Allah.

Hinduism

It came from multiple periods (Pre-vedic to the Modern Hinduism). Hinduism sees the world as both real and not real at the same time.

Buddhism

In Buddhism, the nature and purpose of the world are viewed through the lens of impermanence, suffering, and interconnectedness.

Origin, Nature and Purpose of Humans.
Christianity

The primary purpose of humans is to spread the gospel of God.

Islam

The primary purpose of humans is to live a life pleasing to Allah.

Hinduism

Every person has two parts: Atman which is the soul, and the Body and Mind.

Buddhism

The nature and purpose of humans revolve around understanding the impermanent and dynamic aspects of existence, overcoming suffering through self-transformation and cultivating compassion and wisdom to ultimately achieve liberation from the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth.

Ethics and Morality
Christianity

The scripture is the ultimate standard for christian ethics because God spreads His word and revelation through the scripture.

Islam

Their ethics is based on moral conduct and good character, while their morality is based on the idea of righteousness, good character, and the love of Allah and His creatures.

Hinduism

In Hinduism, there is a distinction made between dharma and karma. Both are essential for the purpose of reaching moksha. Dharma (moral) is the cosmic law or moral principle that governs an individual’s conduct and Karma is the result of past, present and future actions. To achieve good karma (ethical), it is important to live according to dharma. Therefore, in terms of morality in Hinduism, there is an obligation to fulfil one’s destiny as prescribed by the cosmic principle. Karma, on the other hand, serves as the ethical principle in achieving this moral obligation. This distinction is observed in the dilemma, which is played out in the story of Krishna and Arjuna as expressed in the Bhagavad Gita.
Source: Understanding the moral and ethical dimensions of the Bhagavad Gita

Buddhism

The main goal is to reach freedom from suffering by coming to see the world as it actually is and abandoning the distorted projections that our thoughts and emotions create.

The following is their form and structure:

  3. Joint Family System

Buddhism

They have not followed a single social hierarchy. Instead, they live freely.

Buddhism

The afterlife is not a final destination like heaven or hell. It's an ongiong cycle of rebirth.
